PIOPED II evaluated the accuracy of multidetector CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA) for diagnosing acute pulmonary embolism, and the added value of combining CTPA with CT venography. The central lesson is that CTPA accuracy must be interpreted in light of the pretest probability rather than read as a standalone yes/no.

When the clinical (pretest) probability and the CTPA result agree, the test is reliable: a negative CTPA in a low-probability patient effectively rules out PE, and a positive CTPA in a high-probability patient confirms it. The danger zone is discordance, for example a negative CTPA in a high-probability patient or a positive CTPA in a low-probability patient, where the result should not be taken at face value and further testing or clinical judgment is required.

This is a concrete instance of Bayesian reasoning: the same imaging result carries different meaning depending on the prior probability, so a structured pretest probability assessment (for example using a validated clinical decision rule) should precede and frame the imaging.
